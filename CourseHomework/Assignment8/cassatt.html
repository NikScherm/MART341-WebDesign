Mary Cassatt

About Cassatt


Periods: Impressionism and Suffrage

Mary Cassatt (1844–1926) was an American painter and printmaker who spent most of her adult life in France, where she became a prominent figure among the Impressionists. She is known for her depictions of the social and private lives of women, especially focusing on the intimate bonds between mothers and children. Born in Pennsylvania into a wealthy family, she studied art in Philadelphia and later moved to Europe to further her education. Cassatt settled in Paris in 1874, where she developed a close friendship with Edgar Degas, whose influence shaped her work. She exhibited with the Impressionists in the late 19th century, known for her mastery of drawing, use of bright colors, and innovative techniques, including printmaking. Inspired by Japanese art, she produced notable works such as The Child's Bath and The Boating Party. Later in life, Cassatt became an advocate for Impressionist art in America, helping build collections like the H.O. Havemeyer Collection at the Metropolitan Museum of Art. Her failing eyesight forced her to stop working around 1914. Cassatt's legacy lies in both her contributions to Impressionism and her role in shaping American art collections.
